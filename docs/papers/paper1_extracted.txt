Most g existing g imputation g methods g assume g specific g missing g mecha- nisms g in g their g development. g The g underlying g missing g data g mechanism g significantly g influences g the g reliability g and g effectiveness g of g imputation g techniques g ( Kumar g and g Varija , g 2024 ). g Rubin g ( 1976 ) g initially g described g three g types g of g missing g data g mechanisms. g Missing g completely g at g ran- dom g (MCAR) g occurs g when g the g probability g of g missing g data g is g entirely g unrelated g to g any g factors, g with g each g missing g value g arising g purely g by g chance. g Missing g at g random g (MAR) g arises g when g the g probability g of g missing g data g is g unrelated g to g the g value g of g the g missing g data g itself g but g depends g on g other g observed g factors. g For g example, g during g peak g electricity g usage, g monitoring g sites g may g experience g missing g records g due g to g insuffi- cient g power g supply. g Missing g not g at g random g (MNAR) g occurs g when g the g probability g of g missing g data g depends g on g the g value g of g the g missing g data g itself, g or g potentially g on g external g factors, g as g seen g when g excessively g high g PM2.5 g concentrations g cause g particle g accumulation g on g sensor g surfaces, g impairing g the g performance g of g detection g components. g Beyond g these g mechanisms, g two g other g missing g data g patterns g frequently g encountered g in g real-world g applications g are g Missing g in g Sequence g (MIS) g and g Missing g in g Block g (MIB) g ( Du g et g al. , g 2024 ). g For g instance, g in g the g spatiotemporal g data g of g PM2.5 g monitoring g sites, g MIS g refers g to g continuous g missing g data g at g a g single g site g over g a g specific g period, g while g MIB g describes g situations g where g multiple g sites g experience g continuous g missing g data g simultaneously g over g the g same g period. g These g patterns, g characterized g by g their g continuity g and g spatial g correlation, g pose g considerable g challenges g for g data g recovery g and g subsequent g analysis. Traditional g methods g for g missing g values g imputation, g such g as g Multiple g Imputation g by g Chained g Equations g (MICE), g K-Nearest g Neighbors g Impu- tation g (KNNI), g matrix g factorization g (MF), g and g iterative g singular g value g decomposition g (iterative g SVD), g have g been g widely g adopted g due g to g their g solid g theoretical g foundations g and g ease g of g implementation g ( Van g Buuren g and g Oudshoorn , g 2000 ; g Cover g and g Hart , g 1967 ; g Koren g et g al. , g 2009 ; g Troyanskaya g et g al. , g 2001 ). g However, g these g approaches g may g struggle g to g capture g complex g non-linear g relationships g in g real-world g datasets. Recent g studies g have g extensively g explored g missing g values g imputa- tion g to g address g various g missingness g mechanisms g and g feature g char- acteristics, g evolving g from g traditional g statistical g methods g to g more g re- cent g deep g learning-based g models, g such g as g Generative g Adversarial g Net- works g (GANs) g ( Goodfellow g et g al. , g 2014 ). g Miao g et g al. g ( 2021 ) g pro- posed g a g novel g semi-supervised g GAN g (SSGAN), g which g improves g im- putation g performance g for g multivariate g time g series g data g by g integrat- ing g a g semi-supervised g classifier g and g a g temporal g reminder g matrix. g Du g et g al. g ( 2023 ) g proposed g Self-Attention-based g Imputation g for g Time g Series g (SAITS) g for g estimating g missing g values g in g multivariate g time g series, g which g captures g temporal g dependencies g and g feature g correlations g through g two g diagonally-masked g self-attention g (DMSA) g blocks. g Yuan g et g al. g ( 2024 ) g introduced g a g stacked g adaptive g median g iterative g imputation g framework g (SAMIIF) g for g missing g data g recovery g in g time g series. g The g framework g incor- porates g moving g window g and g dynamic g sample g overlay g techniques, g with g an g adaptive g learning g mechanism, g to g iteratively g refine g the g imputation g process. g Yuan g et g al. g ( 2023 ) g proposed g an g attention-based g interval-aided g network g (AIA-Net) g to g impute g missing g values g in g heterogeneous g time g series. g This g method g integrates g attention-based g time-aware g dynamic g im- putation g with g an g interval-aided g time-aware g module g to g model g temporal g dependencies g adaptively. g SSGAN, g SAITS, g and g SAMIIF g are g primarily g limited g to g MCAR-type g missing g data g and g may g degrade g significantly g in g real-world g settings g where g such g assumptions g rarely g hold. g At g the g same g time, g AIA-Net g is g not g well-suited g for g the g MIB g pattern. Hu g et g al. g ( 2020 ) g proposed g a g trinetwork-based g GAN g (tnGAN), g em- ploying g scope-aware g feature g extraction g and g information-aware g feature g integration g to g capture g temporal g evolution g and g spatial g similarity g better. g This g method g may g be g less g effective g in g handling g high g missing g rates, g as g evaluation g was g limited g to g moderate g missing g conditions g (not g exceeding g 33.33%). g Hu g et g al. g ( 2021 ) g proposed g a g hierarchical g GAN-based g data g recovery g method g designed g to g handle g varying g numbers g of g incomplete g data. g A g joint g attention g module g was g introduced g to g extract g spatiotem- poral g dependencies g and g integrate g cross-layer g features. g However, g this g proposed g method g requires g complete g data g pairs g for g training, g which g may g not g always g be g available g in g practice. g Liu g et g al. g ( 2023 ) g proposed g Inverted g Transformers g (iTransformer), g which g applies g attention g and g feed-forward g networks g to g inverted g dimensions. g It g embeds g time g points g into g variable g tokens g to g better g capture g multivariate g correlations g and g non-linear g rep- resentations. g Nonetheless, g its g performance g advantages g may g be g limited g in g scenarios g with g no g external g feature g variables g ( Zhang g and g Thorburn , g 2022 ; g Radii g et g al. , g 2023 ). Che g et g al. g ( 2018 ) g proposed g the g Gated g Recurrent g Unit g with g trainable g Decays g (GRUD), g which g incorporates g a g decay g mechanism g to g model g the g impact g of g missing g values g over g time. g Ou g et g al. g ( 2024 ) g proposed g a g Position-Encoding g Denoising g Autoencoder g (PE-DAE) g for g imputing g missing g data g in g time g series. g This g method g leverages g temporal g autocor- relation g and g the g positions g of g missing g values, g integrating g a g self-paced g learning g training g strategy g to g enhance g performance. g Although g effective g in g practice, g GRUD g and g PE-DAE g involve g model g components g that g must g be g carefully g adapted g to g the g characteristics g of g the g target g data, g making g them g less g flexible g across g diverse g applications. g For g instance, g GRUD g requires g domain-specific g design g of g its g decay g mechanism, g while g PE-DAE g involves g tuning g parameters g for g the g self-paced g learning g strategy, g which g can g be g challenging g in g practice. In g this g study, g we g propose g a g Varying-Coefficient g Autoregressive g Ad- versarial g Network g (VCAAN) g framework g for g the g imputation g of g missing g values g in g spatiotemporal g data. g This g framework g first g utilizes g a g Varying- Coefficient g Autoregressive g model g (VCA) g to g thoroughly g incorporate g the g correlations g between g missing g data g and g historical, g future, g and g surround- ing g information. g By g capturing g the g dynamic g dependencies g inherent g in g spatiotemporal g data, g VCA g also g requires g estimating g fewer g parameters g than g Vector g Autoregressive g (VAR) g ( Sims , g 1980 ). g Subsequently, g we g de- sign g a g Convolutional g Discriminative g Network g (CDN) g to g assess g the g im- puted g missing g values, g training g it g adversarially g alongside g the g VCA. g To g further g enhance g robustness, g we g introduce g a g dynamic g adversarial g loss g weighting g mechanism, g which g mitigates g overfitting g to g a g certain g extent g during g training g (inspired g by g Du g et g al. , g 2023 ; g Huang g et g al. , g 2024 ). g Notably, g the g proposed g method g enables g joint g training g and g imputation g on g incomplete g datasets, g eliminating g the g need g for g separate g training g and g testing g phases. g It g operates g without g requiring g external g feature g variables g and g offers g a g versatile g framework g that g can g be g applied g to g a g wide g range g of g imputation g tasks. g Finally, g a g series g of g experiments g using g the g PM2.5 g concentration g data g in g Beijing g is g conducted g to g validate g the g effectiveness g of g the g proposed g method. g In g particular, g the g main g contributions g of g this g study g are g as g follows. (1) A g novel g hybrid g method g is g proposed g that g integrates g statistical g methods g with g deep g learning g into g a g unified g framework. g This method g provides g enhanced g robustness g and g flexibility, g enabling g effective g imputation g across g diverse g real-world g missing g data g types g and g high g missing g rate g scenarios. (2) A g VCA g module g is g introduced g to g jointly g capture g the g relationships g between g missing g values g and g their g temporal-spatial g context, g in- cluding g historical, g future, g and g adjacent g locations. g The g module g also g effectively g models g the g intrinsic g dynamic g dependencies g in g spatiotemporal g data, g all g within g a g simplified g model g structure. (3) A g CDN g module g is g specifically g designed g for g spatiotemporal g im- putation g and g integrated g with g VCA g through g adversarial g training. g Furthermore, g a g dynamic g adversarial g loss g weighting g mechanism g enhances g adaptability g in g challenging g scenarios. (4) Extensive g experiments g are g performed g to g evaluate g the g performance g of g the g proposed g method g and g competitive g methods. g The g proposed g method g shows g general g advantages g under g various g scenarios.

g Bashir g and g Wei , g 2018 ). g Meanwhile, g GANs, g as g a g powerful g deep g generative g modeling g tech- nique, g face g challenges g in g effectively g capturing g spatiotemporal g patterns g in g missing g values g ( Brimos g et g al. , g 2024 ; g Wang g et g al. , g 2022 ). g Integrat- ing g both g approaches' g strengths g can g largely g improve g the g accuracy g of g imputation g of g the g missing g data. The g proposed g method g incorporates g autoregression g to g capture g the g spatiotemporal g dependencies g of g missing g values, g using g B-spline g functions g to g represent g the g dynamic g coefficients. g Inspired g by g GANs, g we g design g a g Convolutional g Discriminator g Network g to g distinguish g between g real g and g imputed g values g in g the g spatiotemporal g data. g Dynamic g adversarial g loss g weight g is g introduced g in g the g adversarial g training g process g to g adap- tively g adjust g the g weights g of g the g generator g and g discriminator, g effectively g preventing g overfitting. Traditional g imputation g models g typically g require g splitting g the g dataset g into g training g and g testing g sets, g which g has g two g main g drawbacks: g first, g it g limits g data g utilization, g especially g when g data g is g scarce, g potentially g impacting g model g performance; g Second, g for g spatiotemporal g data, g the g simple g temporal g or g spatial g split g may g lead g to g overfitting g due g to g the g complex g dependencies g between g time g and g space g dimensions. g In g con- trast, g the g proposed g model g is g trained g directly g on g the g observed g data g without g splitting, g fully g utilizing g all g available g information g and g avoiding g information g loss. g By g learning g the g overall g distribution g of g spatiotemporal g data g directly g from g the g observed g data, g the g model g can g better g capture g spatiotemporal g dependencies g and g achieve g more g accurate g imputation. g This g approach g simplifies g the g training g process g and g significantly g improves g the g model's g robustness g and g practicality, g making g it g particularly g suitable g for g spatiotemporal g data g imputation g tasks. Extensive g experiments g demonstrate g the g remarkable g potential g of g the g proposed g method. g To g validate g effectiveness g of g our g model, g five g missing g rates g and g five g missingness g patterns g are g considered g in g the g experiments. g Our g model g outperformed g other g methods g under g low, g medium, g and g high g missing g rates g (except g for g MNAR-low). g For g MCAR g and g MAR, g our g model g is g not g sensitive g to g the g initial g imputation g values g and g converges g stably. g However, g under g MNAR g with g medium g and g high g missing g rates, g the g model's g performance g is g kind g of g sensitive g to g the g initial g values g mean- ing g that g the g model g needs g further g improvement. g For g MIS g and g MIB, g the g former g disrupts g long-term g temporal g dependencies, g while g the g latter g preserves g some g local g spatiotemporal g information, g allowing g for g more g accurate g imputation. g Our g model g is g less g stable g in g the g former g scenario g but g significantly g reduces g imputation g loss g (up g to g 81.88% g in g the g upper g quartile g and g 38.93% g in g the g median). The g proposed g method g is g crucial g for g repairing g incomplete g PM2.5 g spatiotemporal g data, g subsequent g exposure g assessment, g and g health g im- pact g studies. g Besides g its g effectiveness g in g the g current g application, g the g proposed g method g is g highly g versatile. g It g can g be g readily g extended g to g other g spatiotemporal g pollutant g data g such g as g NO2, g SO2, g and g O3, g as g well g as g to g other g types g of g correlated g multivariate g time g series. g We g recommend g applying g the g method g to g offline g spatiotemporal g datasets g with g no g more g than g 20 g spatial g monitoring g stations g and g sampling g intervals g higher g than g 15 g min g (e.g., g 15 g min g to g 1 g h g per g observation), g under g which g our g experi- mental g results g demonstrated g stable g and g reliable g performance. g Despite g the g promising g results, g there g is g room g for g improvement g in g handling g spatiotemporal g data g of g large-scale g sites g and g high g missing g rates g under g the g MNAR g mechanism. g In g the g future, g we g will g incorporate g additional g contex- tual g information g (such g as g meteorological g data g and g sensor g status) g and g re- duce g the g model's g reliance g on g pre-estimation g algorithms, g aiming g to g bet- ter g handle g spatiotemporal g data g of g large-scale g sites g and g MNAR g scenarios.
